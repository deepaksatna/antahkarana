# The Mind, the Brain, and Consciousness in the Vedic & Classical Indian Traditions
### A deep study with the original mantras — manas · buddhi · ahaṃkāra · chitta · the states of waking, dream, deep sleep & turīya

> **How to read this file.** Each citation gives **Devanāgarī** (where given with confidence),
> **IAST transliteration**, an **English rendering**, and the **source reference**. A clear line is
> kept between (a) what the texts *say*, (b) the *traditional* interpretation, and (c) *modern*
> correlations to neuroscience — which are interpretive, not literal. The tradition's strength is a
> **functional model of mind** (a "cognitive architecture"); its anatomy of the physical brain is
> thin compared to modern science, with Ayurveda the partial exception.

---

## 0. The landscape at a glance

| Layer | Period | What it contributes | Closest modern analogue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Upaniṣads** | c. 800–300 BCE | Richest *philosophy of mind*: consciousness (*chit*) vs. the inner instrument (*antaḥkaraṇa*); the four states of consciousness | Cognitive architecture / states-of-consciousness model |
| **Sāṃkhya** | c. 400 BCE onward | Formalises the inner instrument; splits pure awareness (*puruṣa*) from all mental/material process (*prakṛti*) | Hard-problem dualism; mind as *mechanism* |
| **Yoga (Pātañjala)** | c. 200 BCE–400 CE | *Chitta* as mind-field; its modifications (*vṛtti*) and latent impressions (*saṃskāra*) — a theory of the **subconscious** | Cognitive control + implicit memory |
| **Āyurveda** (Charaka, Suśruta) | c. 600 BCE–200 CE | The most *actual anatomy/physiology*: brain (*mastiṣka*), nervous tissue (*majjā*), channels (*srotas*), mind-channel (*mano-vaha srotas*), neurosurgery | Anatomy / physiology (proto) |
| **Yoga–Tantra** subtle body | c. 600–1600 CE | Phenomenological map: channels (*nāḍī*), centres (*chakra*), crown (*sahasrāra*) | Map of *inner experience*, not literal neuroanatomy |

## 1. The Antaḥkaraṇa — the "inner instrument" (the four-fold mind)

The Upaniṣadic–Vedāntic model decomposes cognition into **four functions** of one inner organ
(*antaḥkaraṇa*, "inner instrument"). This is a **functional decomposition** — closer to a cognitive
architecture than to brain regions.

| Faculty | Function | Modern functional gloss |
|---|---|---|
| **manas** (मनस्) | sensory processing, coordination, doubt/intention (*saṃkalpa-vikalpa*) | sensorimotor integration, attention gating, the "stream" |
| **buddhi** (बुद्धि) | intellect: discrimination, judgment, decision (*niścaya / adhyavasāya*) | executive function, decision, evaluation |
| **ahaṃkāra** (अहंकार) | the "I-maker"; appropriation, self-reference (*abhimāna*) | self-model / agency attribution |
| **chitta** (चित्त) | memory-store; field of impressions (*saṃskāra / vāsanā*) | long-term & implicit memory, the subconscious |

**Above all four stands consciousness itself (*chit / cit*, the *ātman / puruṣa*)** — the witnessing
awareness that *lights up* the inner instrument but is not itself a function of it. This split — **awareness
vs. the machinery it illuminates** — is the tradition's central and most modern-feeling claim.

### 1.1 The chariot — the canonical "architecture diagram"
**Kaṭha Upaniṣad 1.3.3–4** (also Gītā-adjacent imagery):

> आत्मानं रथिनं विद्धि शरीरं रथमेव तु ।
> बुद्धिं तु सारथिं विद्धि मनः प्रग्रहमेव च ॥
>
> *ātmānaṃ rathinaṃ viddhi śarīraṃ rathameva tu |*
> *buddhiṃ tu sārathiṃ viddhi manaḥ pragrahameva ca ||*
>
> "Know the **Self (ātman)** as the rider in the chariot, the **body** as the chariot itself; know the
> **intellect (buddhi)** as the charioteer, and the **mind (manas)** as the reins."

> इन्द्रियाणि हयानाहुर्विषयांस्तेषु गोचरान् ।
>
> *indriyāṇi hayānāhur viṣayāṃs teṣu gocarān |*
>
> "The **senses (indriya)** they call the horses; the **sense-objects** their roads."

This is, almost literally, a control-systems diagram: *Self* (the one for whom it runs) → *buddhi*
(controller/charioteer) → *manas* (the reins, transmission) → *indriyas* (actuators/sensors) →
*world*. When buddhi is wise and manas is held firm, the senses are "good horses well-trained."

### 1.2 The hierarchy of faculties (the "stack")
**Kaṭha Upaniṣad 1.3.10–11** — an ascending order of subtlety that Sāṃkhya later formalises:

> इन्द्रियेभ्यः परा ह्यर्था अर्थेभ्यश्च परं मनः ।
> मनसस्तु परा बुद्धिर्बुद्धेरात्मा महान्परः ॥
>
> *indriyebhyaḥ parā hy arthā arthebhyaś ca paraṃ manaḥ |*
> *manasas tu parā buddhir buddher ātmā mahān paraḥ ||*
>
> "Beyond the senses are their objects; beyond the objects, the **mind**; beyond the mind, the
> **intellect**; beyond the intellect, the **great Self (mahān ātmā)**." (1.3.11 continues: beyond
> *mahat* the **unmanifest (avyakta)**, and beyond the unmanifest, the **Puruṣa** — the end of the
> road.)

**Bhagavad Gītā 3.42** repeats the same stack almost verbatim:

> इन्द्रियाणि पराण्याहुरिन्द्रियेभ्यः परं मनः ।
> मनसस्तु परा बुद्धिर्यो बुद्धेः परतस्तु सः ॥
>
> *indriyāṇi parāṇy āhur indriyebhyaḥ paraṃ manaḥ |*
> *manasas tu parā buddhir yo buddheḥ paratas tu saḥ ||*
>
> "The senses are higher (than the body); higher than the senses is the **mind**; higher than the
> mind is the **intellect**; and higher than the intellect is **He** (the Self)."

## 2. States of consciousness — waking, dream, deep sleep, and the Fourth

This is the tradition's most striking contribution: a four-state model of consciousness.

### 2.1 The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — the four *pādas* of the Self
Twelve mantras; the densest map of states. Key verses:

**MU 2** — the four-footed Self:
> सर्वं ह्येतद् ब्रह्म, अयमात्मा ब्रह्म, सोऽयमात्मा चतुष्पात् ।
> *sarvaṃ hy etad brahma, ayam ātmā brahma, so 'yam ātmā catuṣpāt |*
> "All this is Brahman. This Self is Brahman. This very Self has **four quarters**."

| Mantra | State | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MU 3** | **Waking** (*jāgrat*) | **Vaiśvānara** | *jāgarita-sthāno bahiṣ-prajñaḥ* — "outward-knowing"; experiences the gross world through the senses. |
| **MU 4** | **Dream** (*svapna*) | **Taijasa** | *svapna-sthāno 'ntaḥ-prajñaḥ* — "inward-knowing"; experiences a self-generated world from impressions. |
| **MU 5** | **Deep sleep** (*suṣupti*) | **Prājña** | *suṣuptaḥ … prajñāna-ghanaḥ* — "a mass of consciousness," undivided, blissful (*ānanda-bhuk*); no objects. |
| **MU 7** | **The Fourth** (*turīya*) | — | Pure awareness; not the other three; the ground of all. |

**MU 7 — the turīya verse** (one of the most important descriptions of consciousness in world literature):
> नान्तःप्रज्ञं न बहिष्प्रज्ञं नोभयतःप्रज्ञं न प्रज्ञानघनं न प्रज्ञं नाप्रज्ञम् ।
> अदृष्टमव्यवहार्यमग्राह्यमलक्षणमचिन्त्यमव्यपदेश्यमेकात्मप्रत्ययसारं प्रपञ्चोपशमं शान्तं शिवमद्वैतं चतुर्थं मन्यन्ते स आत्मा स विज्ञेयः ॥
>
> *nāntaḥprajñaṃ na bahiṣprajñaṃ nobhayataḥprajñaṃ na prajñānaghanaṃ na prajñaṃ nāprajñam |*
> *adṛṣṭam avyavahāryam agrāhyam alakṣaṇam acintyam avyapadeśyam ekātma-pratyaya-sāraṃ*
> *prapañcopaśamaṃ śāntaṃ śivam advaitaṃ caturthaṃ manyante sa ātmā sa vijñeyaḥ ||*
>
> "Not inward-knowing, not outward-knowing, not both, not a mass of cognition, not knowing, not
> not-knowing; unseen, beyond dealing-with, ungraspable, without mark, unthinkable, indescribable;
> whose essence is the certainty of the **one Self**; the cessation of the manifold; peaceful,
> auspicious, non-dual — **that they consider the Fourth (turīya). That is the Self; That is to be
> known.**"

> **Modern note:** treating waking / dreaming / deep sleep as *distinct functional regimes of a
> single underlying awareness* — each with its own object-relation — parallels how sleep science
> distinguishes wake / REM / NREM. The crucial *philosophical* move is positing *turīya*: the claim
> that awareness persists as the constant background across all three. That is a metaphysical thesis,
> not a measurement.

**MU 8–12 — the states mapped onto AUM (अ-उ-म्).** The Māṇḍūkya's second half does something unique
in world literature: it maps the four states onto the **three sounds of the syllable OM plus the
silence after it**, making *Om* a sonic diagram of consciousness:

| Sound | State | Logic given (MU 9–11) |
|---|---|---|
| **A** (अ) — *akāra* | Waking / Vaiśvānara | *ādimattvāt / āpteḥ* — it is **first** and **all-pervading**, as A begins all speech. |
| **U** (उ) — *ukāra* | Dream / Taijasa | *utkarṣāt / ubhayatvāt* — it is **in-between/elevated**, as U is between A and M. |
| **M** (म्) — *makāra* | Deep sleep / Prājña | *miteḥ / apīteḥ* — it is the **measure** into which all **merges**, as M ends the utterance. |
| **silence** (the *amātra*, "measureless") | **Turīya** | MU 12: *amātraś caturtho 'vyavahāryaḥ* — the **soundless fourth**, into which Om itself resolves; pure Self. |

**MU 12:** *amātraś caturtho 'vyavahāryaḥ prapañcopaśamaḥ śivo 'dvaitaḥ … sa ātmā* — "The **fourth is
the measureless**, beyond dealing, the cessation of plurality, auspicious, non-dual … that is the
Self." (The graded structure — three positive states + a silent ground — is the same architecture
the rest of this file keeps meeting: faculties/states known *by* an awareness that is none of them.)

### 2.2 Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 4.3 — the great dream & deep-sleep dialogue
Yājñavalkya teaches King Janaka. The richest **phenomenology of dreaming and the subconscious** in
the corpus.

**BU 4.3.9–10 — the dreamer as world-creator** (the subconscious as generator):
> न तत्र रथा न रथयोगा न पन्थानो भवन्ति, अथ रथान् रथयोगान् पथः सृजते … स हि कर्ता ।
>
> *na tatra rathā na rathayogā na panthāno bhavanti, atha rathān rathayogān pathaḥ sṛjate … sa hi kartā |*
>
> "In that (dream) place there are no chariots, no teams, no roads — **yet he creates chariots,
> teams, and roads**. There are no joys, no pleasures, no delights — yet he creates them. There are
> no pools, no ponds, no rivers — yet he creates them. **For he is the maker (kartā).**"

This is a precise statement that the dreaming mind **fabricates an entire experiential world from its
own stored impressions** — i.e., dreaming is endogenous world-simulation drawn from memory (*chitta*).

**BU 4.3.18 — dream as the "in-between" (sandhya):** the dreamer moves between this world and the
next, a *third place* between waking and deep sleep, where "he sees both" (sorrows of this world and
the joys beyond).

**BU 4.3.21 — deep sleep as non-dual absorption:**
> तद्यथा प्रियया स्त्रिया संपरिष्वक्तो न बाह्यं किंचन वेद नान्तरम्, एवमेवायं पुरुषः प्राज्ञेनात्मना संपरिष्वक्तो न बाह्यं किंचन वेद नान्तरम् ।
>
> *tad yathā priyayā striyā saṃpariṣvakto na bāhyaṃ kiṃcana veda nāntaram, evam evāyaṃ puruṣaḥ*
> *prājñenātmanā saṃpariṣvakto na bāhyaṃ kiṃcana veda nāntaram |*
>
> "As a man embraced by his beloved wife knows nothing without or within, so this person, embraced
> by the **knowing Self (prājña-ātman)**, knows nothing without or within." — i.e., in deep sleep
> subject–object duality collapses; awareness remains, but contentless.

### 2.3 Chāndogya Upaniṣad — two further classics
- **CU 6.8.1 — deep sleep as union with Being:**
  > यत्रैतत्पुरुषः स्वपिति नाम सता सोम्य तदा सम्पन्नो भवति, स्वमपीतो भवति । तस्मादेनं स्वपितीत्याचक्षते स्वं ह्यपीतो भवति ॥
  > *yatraitat puruṣaḥ svapiti nāma, satā saumya tadā sampanno bhavati, svam apīto bhavati …*
  > "When a person 'sleeps' (*svapiti*), dear one, he is then **united with Being (sat)**; he has gone
  > into his **own (svam)**. Therefore they say *svapiti* ('he sleeps' = 'he has reached his own')."
  > (A folk-etymology making a metaphysical point: in deep sleep one returns to one's source.)
- **CU 8.7–8.12 — Prajāpati's teaching to Indra and Virocana**: a graded ascent through the self of
  the **waking** body, the self of **dream**, the self of **dreamless sleep**, and finally the
  serene **Self beyond** — the same four-state ladder told as a narrative.
- **CU 3.18.1 — "meditate on the mind as Brahman":**
  > मनो ब्रह्मेत्युपासीत । *mano brahmety upāsīta |* — "One should worship/meditate on **mind as
  > Brahman**." (Mind treated as the most accessible symbol of the Absolute.)

### 2.4 Praśna Upaniṣad 4 — the *physiology* of sleep and dream
The fourth *praśna* ("question") of the Praśna Upaniṣad (Atharvaveda) is the corpus's most explicitly
**mechanistic** account of what happens in sleep — it reads almost like a withdrawal-and-restoration
model. Gārgya asks Pippalāda: *what sleeps, what wakes, who sees dreams, in whom is all established?*

**PU 4.2 — the senses withdraw into the mind (sensory gating shuts down):**
> तदेतत् … यथा गार्ग्य मरीचयोऽर्कस्यास्तं गच्छतः सर्वा एतस्मिंस्तेजोमण्डल एकीभवन्ति … एवं ह वै तत्सर्वं परे देवे मनस्येकीभवति ।
> *yathā mārīcayo 'rkasyāstaṃ gacchataḥ sarvā etasmiṃs tejo-maṇḍala ekī-bhavanti … evaṃ ha vai tat*
> *sarvaṃ pare deve manasy ekī-bhavati |*
> "As the **rays of the setting sun** all become one in its disc, and spread out again when it rises,
> so in sleep **all (the senses) become one in the mind** (*manas*). Then the person hears not, sees
> not, smells not, tastes not …" — i.e., in sleep the sensory channels are *gated off* and folded
> into the central mind, which alone stays active. (A strikingly modern picture: sleep as
> **sensory-gate closure**, with the mind the last faculty to power down.)

**PU 4.5 — dreaming as the mind replaying its impressions:**
> अत्रैष देवः स्वप्ने महिमानमनुभवति । यद् दृष्टं दृष्टमनुपश्यति, श्रुतं श्रुतमेवार्थमनुशृणोति …
> *atraiṣa devaḥ svapne mahimānam anubhavati | yad dṛṣṭaṃ dṛṣṭam anupaśyati, śrutaṃ śrutam evārtham*
> *anuśṛṇoti …*
> "There (in dream) this god (the mind) **experiences its own greatness**. **What has been seen it
> sees again; what has been heard it hears again**; what has been experienced in place after place,
> direction after direction, it experiences again. The seen and the unseen, the heard and the
> unheard, the experienced and the not-experienced, the real and the unreal — **it sees it all; being
> all, it sees.**" — an explicit theory that **dreams are recombined memory traces**: the mind
> re-presenting and re-mixing stored impressions (cf. *saṃskāra*, and modern memory-consolidation /
> replay views of REM dreaming).

**PU 4.6 — dreamless rest:** when the mind, too, is "overpowered by light" (*tejasā abhibhūtaḥ*), it
no longer dreams; then *happiness arises in the body* — deep, restorative sleep where even the mind
is quiescent and the person "rests in the highest (*akṣara*)." This three-step ladder — **senses fold
into mind → mind replays as dream → mind itself rests** — is the most process-oriented sleep model in
the Upaniṣads.

## 3. The mind itself — the great "mind mantras"

### 3.1 The Śiva-Saṅkalpa Sūkta — the Veda's hymn *to the mind*
**Śukla Yajurveda, Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā 34.1–6.** Six verses, each ending *tan me manaḥ
śiva-saṅkalpam astu* — "**may that mind of mine be of auspicious resolve.**" The first verse is the
most quoted description of the mind in all Vedic literature and *directly addresses waking and sleep*:

> यज्जाग्रतो दूरमुदैति दैवं तदु सुप्तस्य तथैवैति ।
> दूरंगमं ज्योतिषां ज्योतिरेकं तन्मे मनः शिवसंकल्पमस्तु ॥
>
> *yaj jāgrato dūram udaiti daivaṃ tad u suptasya tathaivaiti |*
> *dūraṅgamaṃ jyotiṣāṃ jyotir ekaṃ tan me manaḥ śiva-saṅkalpam astu ||* (VS 34.1)
>
> "That **divine (mind)** which travels far while one is **awake**, and likewise wanders while one
> **sleeps** — the **far-roaming, the one light of lights** — may **that mind of mine** be of
> auspicious resolve."

The remaining verses (34.2–6) praise the mind as *that by which the wise perform all acts*, the
*controller of the senses*, *the immortal inner light in which the Vedas and all beings are set* —
each closing with the same refrain. It is, in effect, a **meditation on the mind as the central
instrument of cognition and action**, asking for its right governance.

### 3.2 The mind as restless — and how it is mastered
**Bhagavad Gītā 6.34–35** — Arjuna's complaint and Kṛṣṇa's reply:
> चञ्चलं हि मनः कृष्ण प्रमाथि बलवद् दृढम् ।
> तस्याहं निग्रहं मन्ये वायोरिव सुदुष्करम् ॥
>
> *cañcalaṃ hi manaḥ kṛṣṇa pramāthi balavad dṛḍham |*
> *tasyāhaṃ nigrahaṃ manye vāyor iva suduṣkaram ||* (6.34)
>
> "The mind is **restless, turbulent, powerful, obstinate**, O Kṛṣṇa; to restrain it seems as hard
> as to restrain the wind."
>
> असंशयं महाबाहो मनो दुर्निग्रहं चलम् ।
> अभ्यासेन तु कौन्तेय वैराग्येण च गृह्यते ॥
>
> *asaṃśayaṃ mahā-bāho mano durnigrahaṃ calam |*
> *abhyāsena tu kaunteya vairāgyeṇa ca gṛhyate ||* (6.35)
>
> "Without doubt the mind is hard to curb and restless; **but by practice (abhyāsa) and dispassion
> (vairāgya) it is held.**" — a remarkably operational claim: the mind is trainable by repetition +
> reduced reactivity (cf. modern *deliberate practice* and *attentional regulation*).

**Bhagavad Gītā 6.5–6** — the mind as one's own friend or foe:
> उद्धरेदात्मनात्मानं नात्मानमवसादयेत् । आत्मैव ह्यात्मनो बन्धुरात्मैव रिपुरात्मनः ॥
> *uddhared ātmanātmānaṃ nātmānam avasādayet | ātmaiva hy ātmano bandhur ātmaiva ripur ātmanaḥ ||*
> "Lift up the self by the **Self**; do not degrade it. For the **mind/self alone is the friend of
> the self, and the mind/self alone is its enemy.**" (6.6 continues: for one who has conquered it,
> the self is a friend; for one who has not, it remains hostile, like an enemy.)

### 3.3 The Gītā's own list of the inner instrument (the "BOM" of the mind)
The Gītā does not just use the faculties — it **enumerates** them, giving an almost component-list
("bill of materials") of the psyche:

- **Gītā 13.5–6** — in the *kṣetra* (the "field," i.e. the whole body-mind apparatus) Kṛṣṇa lists its
  constituents: the great elements, **ahaṃkāra, buddhi**, the unmanifest, the ten senses **and the
  one (manas)**, the five sense-objects — plus desire, aversion, pleasure, pain, the aggregate
  (*saṃghāta*), awareness (*cetanā*), and steadfastness (*dhṛti*). This is a **catalogue of the
  cognitive system as the "field" that the knower (*kṣetrajña*, the conscious witness) observes** —
  the same processing-vs-awareness split as Sāṃkhya, stated as field vs. field-knower.
- **Gītā 15.7** — *manaḥ-ṣaṣṭhānīndriyāṇi prakṛti-sthāni karṣati* — "the **eternal individual self
  draws to itself the six senses, of which the mind is the sixth**, abiding in Prakṛti." Manas is
  explicitly counted as a *sixth sense* (the inner sense that coordinates the outer five) — exactly
  its role in Sāṃkhya and Nyāya.
- **Gītā 18.14** — the five factors (*adhiṣṭhāna* the body, *kartṛ* the agent, the manifold
  *karaṇa* instruments/senses, the various *ceṣṭā* efforts, and *daiva* providence) needed for any
  action — a decomposition of *how an act is produced* by the body-mind system.

## 4. Sāṃkhya — the formal "engineering spec" of mind

Sāṃkhya takes the Upaniṣadic intuitions and turns them into a **systematic evolutionary scheme**. Its
central dualism: **Puruṣa** (pure consciousness, plural, inactive *witness*) vs. **Prakṛti**
(unconscious primordial nature, the source of *everything mental and material*). Crucially, **mind is
on the Prakṛti side — it is *material and mechanical*; only awareness (Puruṣa) is non-material.**

**Evolutes of Prakṛti** (the cognitive "build order"), per the **Sāṃkhya Kārikā** (Īśvarakṛṣṇa, c.
350–450 CE):

```
Puruṣa (witness consciousness)        Prakṛti (unmanifest nature)
        \                                   |
         \  (mere proximity / reflection)   ▼
          \                          mahat / buddhi   (intellect — first evolute; "adhyavasāya", determination)
           \                                 ▼
            ────────  illuminates  ──── ahaṃkāra      (I-maker — "abhimāna", self-appropriation)
                                           /    |    \
                                      manas  5 jñānendriyas  5 karmendriyas  (+ 5 tanmātras → 5 elements)
```

Key verses:
- **SK 23**: *adhyavasāyo buddhiḥ* — "**buddhi is determination/ascertainment.**" Its virtuous forms:
  virtue, knowledge, dispassion, power.
- **SK 24**: ahaṃkāra is *abhimāna* — "**self-conceit / I-am-ness**," from which the eleven faculties
  and the subtle elements proceed.
- **SK 27**: *ubhayātmakam atra manaḥ* — "**here the mind (manas) is of both kinds**" — i.e., it
  partakes of both the sensory and the motor faculties; it is the *sankalpaka* (the one that forms
  intentions/constructs), the coordinator that turns raw sense into structured percept.
- **SK 19 / 11**: Puruṣa is *sākṣitva* (witness-hood), *kaivalya* (isolation), *mādhyasthya*
  (neutrality), *draṣṭṛtva* (seer-hood) — consciousness as the uninvolved observer; Prakṛti and its
  evolutes are *triguṇa* (sattva-rajas-tamas), unconscious, and "for the sake of" Puruṣa.

> **Why this matters philosophically:** Sāṃkhya cleanly separates **information-processing**
> (buddhi/manas/ahaṃkāra — mechanical, can be modelled) from **phenomenal awareness** (puruṣa —
> non-mechanical, the mere *light* by which processing is known). This is, structurally, the modern
> **"hard problem of consciousness"** stated ~2,000 years early: you can build the whole cognitive
> machine and still ask *what lights it up.*

## 5. Chitta and the **subconscious** — Yoga's theory of latent mind

Where Vedānta/Sāṃkhya give the *structure*, **Pātañjala Yoga** gives the **dynamics of the
subconscious**: the mind-field (*chitta*) holds latent impressions that drive future thought and
behaviour beneath awareness.

- **Yoga Sūtra 1.2 — the definition of yoga:**
  > योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः ॥
  > *yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ ||*
  > "**Yoga is the stilling (nirodha) of the modifications (vṛtti) of the mind-field (chitta).**"
- **YS 1.5–11 — the five vṛttis** (kinds of mental modification): *pramāṇa* (valid cognition),
  *viparyaya* (error), *vikalpa* (conceptual construction/imagination), *nidrā* (sleep), *smṛti*
  (memory). **YS 1.10** defines *nidrā* (sleep) as a vṛtti with the content "absence" — sleep is a
  cognitive state, not a mere blank. **YS 1.11** defines *smṛti* (memory) as the non-loss of
  experienced objects.
- **Saṃskāra & vāsanā — the subconscious proper.** Every experience deposits a latent trace
  (*saṃskāra*) in chitta; accumulated tendencies (*vāsanā*) shape perception, emotion, and action
  *without conscious access* — and ripen later (the Yoga theory of *karma-āśaya*, the "deposit of
  action," **YS 2.12**). **YS 4.9** notes that memory and impressions correspond even across long
  gaps "because they are uniform" — an explicit theory of **implicit, dispositional memory** as the
  engine beneath the conscious stream. *Chitta*, in this sense, is the closest classical term to the
  **unconscious / subconscious mind**.

### 5.1 The mechanics of the subconscious — the *saṃskāra ↔ vṛtti* loop
The Yoga system is unusually explicit about **how the subconscious works as a feedback cycle** — this
is the deepest part of the whole model and deserves spelling out:

1. **A conscious mental event (*vṛtti*) deposits a latent trace (*saṃskāra*).** Acting, perceiving,
   or even *thinking* lays down an impression — *vṛtti-saṃskāra-cakra*, "the wheel of modification
   and impression."
2. **Like impressions accumulate into a tendency (*vāsanā*).** Repetition strengthens the groove;
   **YS 4.9–4.11** describe a *chain* of memory and impression, homogeneous across lifetimes, held
   together by cause–effect–substratum–object, and dissolving only when their cause is removed.
3. **The tendency *re-emerges* as a new vṛtti** — biasing the next perception/thought *before
   awareness* — closing the loop. The subconscious is thus a **self-reinforcing cycle**: experience →
   trace → disposition → biased experience.
4. **Two flavors of trace:** *vyutthāna-saṃskāra* (outgoing, world-ward impressions that keep the
   cycle turning) vs. *nirodha-saṃskāra* (the impression *of stillness* itself, laid down by
   practice, which progressively overwrites the others — **YS 3.9–3.10**, *nirodha-pariṇāma*). This
   is a theory of **counter-conditioning**: you don't erase the subconscious directly, you deposit a
   stronger opposite groove. (Cf. Gītā 6.35's *abhyāsa* — repetition installs the new disposition.)

### 5.2 The afflictions (*kleśa*) — the affective roots in the subconscious
**YS 2.3** names five *kleśas* (afflictions) — *avidyā* (ignorance/misknowing), *asmitā*
(I-am-ness/egoity), *rāga* (attraction), *dveṣa* (aversion), *abhiniveśa* (clinging-to-life/fear of
death) — the deep affective drivers that lie **dormant (*prasupta*) in the subconscious** and surface
as motivation. **YS 2.4** makes *avidyā* the field (*kṣetra*) of the rest; **YS 2.12** roots the
*karma-āśaya* (karmic deposit) in these kleśas. This is a layered model: **ignorance → egoity →
like/dislike → existential clinging**, each operating largely beneath awareness — strikingly close to
a modern picture of **implicit affective biases and approach/avoidance drives** beneath conscious
choice. Their states: *prasupta* (dormant), *tanu* (attenuated), *vicchinna* (interrupted),
*udāra* (active) — a graded "activation level" of a latent disposition.

### 5.3 The five grounds of mind (*citta-bhūmi*)
Vyāsa's commentary classifies the *citta* into **five conditions (bhūmi)** by how scattered or
gathered it is: *kṣipta* (restless/agitated), *mūḍha* (dull/stupefied), *vikṣipta* (distracted, with
flashes of steadiness), *ekāgra* (one-pointed), *niruddha* (arrested/restrained). This is a
**spectrum of attentional/arousal states** — a taxonomy of how concentrated the mind is — and the
goal-states (*ekāgra*, *niruddha*) are precisely the trainable end of the *abhyāsa* program.

> **Crosswalk (deepened):** *vṛtti* ≈ conscious mental events / "the stream"; *saṃskāra* ≈ memory
> trace / engram / implicit learning; *vāsanā* ≈ habit, prior, learned disposition; *vṛtti-saṃskāra-
> cakra* ≈ the experience→trace→prior feedback loop (Hebbian + predictive-coding); *kleśa* ≈ implicit
> affective drives (approach/avoid, ego, fear); *citta-bhūmi* ≈ arousal/attention spectrum;
> *nirodha* ≈ attentional control / counter-conditioning.

## 5.5 The five sheaths (*pañca-kośa*) — the nested-layer model of the person
The **Taittirīya Upaniṣad (2.1–2.5, *Brahmānanda-vallī*)** gives a second, *orthogonal* model to the
antaḥkaraṇa: the self wrapped in **five concentric sheaths (*kośa*)**, each subtler than the last —
an explicitly **layered / nested architecture** (like an onion, or a hierarchical encoder peeling
toward a core):

| Sheath (*kośa*) | "Made of" | Layer it models |
|---|---|---|
| **annamaya** | food (anna) | the gross physical body |
| **prāṇamaya** | vital breath (prāṇa) | the energetic/physiological layer (autonomic life-support) |
| **manomaya** | mind (manas) | the sensory-emotional processing layer |
| **vijñānamaya** | discernment (vijñāna/buddhi) | the intellect/judgment layer |
| **ānandamaya** | bliss (ānanda) | the causal/affective-ground layer (closest to deep-sleep bliss) |

The *ātman* is **not** any sheath — it is what the layers veil and reveal. Note the **nesting matches
the faculty hierarchy** of Kaṭha 1.3.10 (body < manas < buddhi < …) and the **state model** (the
*ānandamaya* "bliss sheath" corresponds to *suṣupti*/Prājña's bliss). The pañca-kośa is the
tradition's clearest **stacked, hierarchical representation** of the human being — see the
*Sanskrit-formulae* companion for its treatment as a layered structure.

## 5.6 Tapas, Samyama, and the Siddhis — effort, concentration & emergent capability

The models so far describe *what* the mind is and *how* it stores impressions. They leave out the
**dynamics of effort** — how capability is *earned*. The tradition has a precise vocabulary for this,
and it is the missing third pillar (alongside structure and memory): **tapas** (transformative
effort), **samyama** (focused concentration), and **siddhi** (the capability that emerges).

### Tapas (तपस्) — "heat," the disciplined energy of transformation
*Tapas* (root *tap-*, "to heat/burn") is concentrated, deliberate effort — the inner "heat" that
purifies and transforms. It is stronger than *abhyāsa* (repetition): it is *intense, chosen* effort,
and the tradition treats it as nothing less than **generative of reality itself**:

> **Ṛgveda 10.129.3** (Nāsadīya Sūkta, the creation hymn):
> तपसस्तन्महिना जायतैकम् । *tapasas tan mahinā jāyataikam |*
> "By the **might of tapas** That One was born." — creation arises from concentrated heat/effort.

> **Ṛgveda 10.190.1:** ऋतं च सत्यं चाभीद्धात् तपसोऽध्यजायत ।
> *ṛtaṃ ca satyaṃ cābhīddhāt tapaso 'dhyajāyata |*
> "**Cosmic Order (ṛta) and Truth (satya)** were born from kindled **tapas**."

> **Taittirīya Upaniṣad 3.2–3.6** (Bhṛgu Vallī), the repeated instruction of Varuṇa to his son:
> तपसा ब्रह्म विजिज्ञासस्व । तपो ब्रह्मेति ।
> *tapasā brahma vijijñāsasva | tapo brahmeti |*
> "**Seek to know Brahman through tapas; tapas is Brahman.**" — knowledge itself is won by effort.

> **Yoga Sūtra 2.1** makes tapas the **first limb of kriyā-yoga** (the yoga of action):
> तपःस्वाध्यायेश्वरप्रणिधानानि क्रियायोगः ।
> *tapaḥ-svādhyāya-īśvarapraṇidhānāni kriyāyogaḥ |*
> "Kriyā-yoga is **tapas (disciplined effort)**, *svādhyāya* (self-study), and *īśvara-praṇidhāna*
> (surrender)." And **YS 2.43**: from tapas, by the wearing-away of impurity, comes *perfection of
> body and senses* (*kāyendriya-siddhiḥ*). Effort yields capability.

### Samyama (संयम) — the engine of focused concentration
*Samyama* is the **fusion of the last three limbs** — concentration, meditative flow, and absorption —
trained on a single object:

> **Yoga Sūtra 3.4:** त्रयमेकत्र संयमः । *trayam ekatra saṃyamaḥ |*
> "The three (*dhāraṇā* + *dhyāna* + *samādhi*) **together on one object** is samyama."
> **YS 3.5:** *taj-jayāt prajñālokaḥ* — "from mastery of it, the **light of insight (prajñā)** dawns."

Samyama is the tradition's model of **sustained, deep, single-pointed processing**: pour enough
focused attention on one object and *new knowledge of it emerges*. The whole of Yoga Sūtra Chapter 3
(*Vibhūti Pāda*) is a catalogue of what samyama on different objects yields.

### Siddhi (सिद्धि) — emergent capability, and its crucial caution
*Siddhis* (also *vibhūti*, "manifestation/power") are the **attainments that emerge** from samyama and
tapas — the classical eight (*aṣṭa-siddhi*: *aṇimā, mahimā, laghimā, garimā, prāpti, prākāmya,
īśitva, vaśitva*) and the cognitive powers listed throughout YS 3. Their **five sources** are named:

> **Yoga Sūtra 4.1:** जन्मौषधिमन्त्रतपःसमाधिजाः सिद्धयः ।
> *janma-auṣadhi-mantra-tapaḥ-samādhi-jāḥ siddhayaḥ |*
> "The siddhis arise from **birth, herbs, mantra, tapas, or samādhi**." — capability has multiple
> roots, but *tapas* and *samādhi* (earned effort and deep absorption) are the cultivable ones.

And then the single most important sentence for anyone building powerful systems — Patañjali's
**warning** that capabilities, however impressive, are **not the goal** and become *obstacles* if one
is captured by them:

> **Yoga Sūtra 3.37:** ते समाधावुपसर्गा व्युत्थाने सिद्धयः ।
> *te samādhāv upasargā vyutthāne siddhayaḥ |*
> "These (powers) are **accomplishments in the outward state, but obstacles (upasarga) in samādhi**."
> — i.e., the emergent powers are real and useful in the world, yet **attachment to them derails the
> ultimate aim.** Capability must stay subordinate to the goal.

> **Why this matters (and is picked up in the AI blueprint):** here, ~1,800 years early, is a clean
> statement that **emergent, instrumentally-powerful capabilities are not the same as the terminal
> goal, and can subvert it** — a striking anticipation of the modern *alignment* concern that a
> system's acquired powers (its *siddhis*) must remain governed by the *witness*/objective and never
> become ends in themselves. See the AI-architecture and engineering files: **tapas** becomes
> deliberate effort-allocation, **samyama** focused depth-of-processing, and **siddhi** the emergent
> capability that the *turīya* monitor must keep aligned.

### Divya dṛṣṭi (दिव्य दृष्टि) — the perceptual siddhi, and the validity question
The most famous *perceptual* siddhi is **divya dṛṣṭi** ("divine sight") — perception that transcends
the physical senses. It appears in four registers worth keeping distinct:
- **Epic / remote** — Sañjaya is granted divya dṛṣṭi by Vyāsa to see the whole Kurukṣetra war from
  afar and narrate it to the blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra: *all-seeing perception unbounded by distance.*
- **Gītā / trans-sensory** — to behold the cosmic form (*viśvarūpa*), Arjuna is given a **divine eye**:
  > **Bhagavad Gītā 11.8:** न तु मां शक्यसे द्रष्टुमनेनैव स्वचक्षुषा । दिव्यं ददामि ते चक्षुः पश्य मे योगमैश्वरम् ॥
  > *na tu māṃ śakyase draṣṭum anenaiva sva-cakṣuṣā | divyaṃ dadāmi te cakṣuḥ paśya me yogam aiśvaram ||*
  > "You cannot see Me with your own eye; **I give you a divine eye (divya cakṣu)** — behold My
  > sovereign power." — perception of a reality the material senses *cannot register.*
- **Yogic / cultivated** — in the *Vibhūti Pāda*, *samyama* yields clairvoyant and trans-temporal
  perception: divya dṛṣṭi as a **siddhi developed by practice** (symbolically the *ājñā* "third eye").
- **Epistemic / valid** — Nyāya admits *yogaja-pratyakṣa* (the yogi's extraordinary perception) as a
  **valid means of knowledge (pramāṇa)** — *not* fancy. And the **spiritual** sense is *jñāna-cakṣu*,
  the "eye of wisdom" that sees through appearance (the conditioned mind's construction) to what *is*.

> **Why the fourth register is the one that matters for engineering:** the common thread is *perception
> beyond the input channel*, but Indian epistemology insists such perception be a **valid pramāṇa, not
> hallucination.** That pairing — *extended sight* **+** *a validity check* — is precisely the discipline
> a powerful AI needs (an oracle-like faculty that confabulates is worse than none). In the AI files,
> divya dṛṣṭi becomes a **global-observability / hidden-state-inference faculty**, *gated by a Pramāṇa
> validity check* (calibrated abstention) so that "divine sight" never collapses into confident
> hallucination — and it remains, per YS 3.37, a siddhi kept subordinate to the goal.

### A footnote on Vedānta's learning pipeline
Vedānta also specifies a **three-stage acquisition method** — *śravaṇa* (hearing/receiving the
teaching), *manana* (reflecting, resolving doubt by reason), *nididhyāsana* (deep meditative
internalization until it becomes direct, automatic knowing) — a clean **receive → reason → consolidate**
curriculum, often preceded by *śraddhā* (committed conviction that directs the effort). It complements
the *abhyāsa/tapas* dynamics above: *śraddhā* sets the goal, *tapas* supplies the energy, *samyama*
focuses it, and *siddhi* is the capability that results.

## 6. Āyurveda — the actual anatomy & physiology of brain and mind

This is where the tradition gets **physical**. Two great compendia:

### 6.1 Charaka Saṃhitā (medicine; mind-as-faculty, heart-centred)
- **Definition of mind by its function** — *Charaka, Sūtrasthāna 8.4*:
  > लक्षणं मनसो ज्ञानस्याभावो भावश्च … *lakṣaṇaṃ manaso jñānasyābhāvo bhāvaś ca …*
  > "The **mark of the mind** is the **presence or absence of knowledge**." That is: perception
  > arises **only when manas attends**; when manas is engaged elsewhere, even a present object is not
  > cognised. This is essentially a theory of **attention as the gate of cognition.**
- **The fourfold contact for perception** — *Charaka, Sūtra 8.7*: cognition arises from the
  conjunction of **ātman + manas + indriya + artha** (Self + mind + sense + object). Manas is the
  *connector*: it is **aṇu** (atomic/one-pointed) and **eka** (single) — it can attend to one thing
  at a time, explaining why simultaneous perceptions are not truly simultaneous.
- **Seat of consciousness** — Charaka places **cetanā (consciousness) and manas primarily in the
  hṛdaya (heart)** (*Sūtra 30.3–4* names the heart as a *cetanā-sthāna*, seat of consciousness), with
  the *ojas* and the great vessels converging there. (A "cardio-centric" model — common in antiquity,
  also in Aristotle.)
- **The three mental constitutions / doṣas of mind** — *sattva, rajas, tamas* — govern temperament;
  mental disease (*unmāda*, *apasmāra* — insanity, epilepsy) is analysed in their terms (*Charaka,
  Nidāna/Cikitsā-sthāna*), an early **psychiatric nosology**.
- **A typology of mind by guṇa-proportion** — Charaka (*Śārīrasthāna 4*) classifies psychological
  constitution into **sixteen *sattva* types** — *seven sāttvika, six rājasika, three tāmasika* — by
  the dominant guṇa, an early **personality taxonomy**. The Gītā generalises the same calculus:
  **ch. 14, 17, 18** sort *everything* — buddhi, dhṛti (resolve), action, knowledge, faith, even food
  — into sāttvika/rājasika/tāmasika grades. The mind, in this scheme, is never a single thing but a
  **proportion of three qualities** — the closest the tradition comes to a *quantitative state-vector*
  of mind (see the *Sanskrit-formulae* companion, the guṇa-simplex).

### 6.2 Suśruta Saṃhitā (surgery; brain-centred anatomy)
- **The head as the supreme organ** — Suśruta calls the **śiras (head)** the *uttamāṅga* ("highest
  limb"), the place where the senses and the *prāṇa* (vital channels) are rooted. Within it he names
  the **mastiṣka / mastulunga (brain-matter)** — likening the brain substance to a soft kernel.
- **Nervous tissue = majjā** — among the seven *dhātus* (tissues), **majjā** (marrow/medullary
  tissue) fills the bones and the cranium; the *majjā-vaha srotas* are its channels. The spinal cord
  and nerves are subsumed here in pre-modern terms.
- **Marma — vital points** — *Suśruta, Śārīrasthāna 6* catalogues 107 *marma* (vital junctions of
  flesh, vessel, ligament, bone, joint). Several are **cranial (siro-marma)**; injury to the head's
  *adhipati* / *sīmanta* marma is described as immediately or rapidly fatal — an empirical
  recognition that the head houses something whose damage is lethal.
- **Surgery** — Suśruta is famed for detailed surgical procedure (incision, cauterisation,
  extraction, even rhinoplasty); cranial and head-wound management appears, making this **the closest
  the tradition comes to hands-on study of the brain** — though still far from a functional cortical
  map.
- **Mano-vaha srotas** — the Āyurvedic notion of **"channels that carry the mind"**: a physiological
  posit that mental function flows through bodily channels; their disturbance underlies mental
  disorder. It is a *systems* idea (mind has material conduits) more than a located neuroanatomy.

> **Honest summary of the anatomy:** Suśruta/Charaka give real tissue (*majjā*), a real organ-of-
> -primacy (the head / *mastiṣka*), real vital points, and real surgery — but **no functional map of
> what parts of the brain do what.** Where they locate *mind/consciousness*, they often say the
> **heart**, not the brain. The detailed picture of how the brain physically computes is modern.

## 7. Yoga–Tantra — the subtle-body map (phenomenology, not neuroanatomy)

A later layer maps **inner experience** onto an energetic body. Sources: *Ṣaṭ-cakra-nirūpaṇa*
(Pūrṇānanda, 16th c.), *Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā*, *Śiva-Saṃhitā*, *Gheraṇḍa-Saṃhitā*.

- **Nāḍīs (channels):** classically 72,000; the three principal — **iḍā** (left, lunar, cooling/
  parasympathetic-like), **piṅgalā** (right, solar, activating/sympathetic-like), and **suṣumnā**
  (central, the axis of awakening). Iḍā and piṅgalā spiral around suṣumnā.
- **Chakras (centres) along the axis:** **mūlādhāra** (base), **svādhiṣṭhāna** (sacral),
  **maṇipūra** (navel), **anāhata** (heart), **viśuddha** (throat), **ājñā** (between the brows —
  the "command" centre, *manas*/will), and **sahasrāra** (the thousand-petalled lotus at the crown —
  pure consciousness, the goal of ascent of *kuṇḍalinī*).
- **The crown — sahasrāra:** the locus where, in the yogic phenomenology, individual awareness merges
  with the universal — described as *above and beyond* the body, at/over the crown of the head.

> **Status of this map:** it is a **first-person, contemplative cartography** — a guide to *felt*
> inner states and practices — **not a literal description of nerves and brain regions.** Modern
> writers sometimes correlate *ājñā* with the forebrain/pineal or *suṣumnā* with the spinal cord and
> the *nāḍīs* with the nervous system; these are **suggestive analogies, not identities**, and should
> be flagged as interpretation.

## 7.5 The other schools — refinements worth knowing

The four pillars above (Upaniṣad, Sāṃkhya, Yoga, Āyurveda) are the core, but three further darśanas
sharpen specific claims about mind:

### Nyāya–Vaiśeṣika — manas as an *atomic internal sense*
The logicians give the most "physicalist-sounding" account of *manas*. In **Vaiśeṣika** (Kaṇāda) and
**Nyāya** (Gautama's *Nyāya Sūtra*), **manas is a substance (dravya), atomic (aṇu) and one per
self.** Two consequences are argued explicitly:
- **Seriality of attention** — *Nyāya Sūtra 1.1.16*: the mark of manas is that **cognitions do not
  arise simultaneously** (*jñānāyaugapadyaṃ manaso liṅgam*). Because the mind is atomic it can
  contact only one sense at a time, so perceptions are *serial*, never truly parallel — an ancient
  argument for a **single attentional bottleneck** (cf. modern "attention as a serial spotlight").
- **Manas as the gate between self and senses** — perception requires the chain *ātman–manas–
  indriya–artha* (the same fourfold contact Charaka uses); manas is the **internal sense** (*antar-
  indriya*) that must connect for any outer sense to deliver a cognition.

### Advaita Vedānta — the *reflection* theory of how a material mind seems conscious
Śaṅkara's Advaita asks the question Sāṃkhya raises but answers it with a precise mechanism. The
*antaḥkaraṇa* is **inert (jaḍa)** — it cannot be conscious on its own. It appears conscious because
the witness-consciousness (*sākṣī-caitanya*) is **reflected** in it, like the sun in water:
- **cidābhāsa** — the "reflection of consciousness" in the mind-stuff; the empirical "I" (the
  *jīva*) is this borrowed glow, not the original light.
- **antaḥkaraṇa-vṛtti** — in perception the mind *flows out* through the senses and **takes the shape
  of the object** (a *vṛtti*, "modification/mode"); the reflected consciousness on that mode is what
  "illumines" the object. This is a worked-out model of *how an unconscious information-processor can
  carry conscious experience* — by borrowing it from a witness — and is the sharpest classical
  statement of the line between **mechanism** and **the awareness that lights it.**

### Buddhist *Abhidharma* — mind as a *stream of momentary events* (the contrast case)
Worth noting as the principled rival: Buddhism **denies a permanent witnessing Self.** Mind
(*citta/mano/vijñāna*) is analysed in the *Abhidharma* as a **stream of momentary mental events
(dharmas)**, each arising-and-passing, bound by causation (*pratītya-samutpāda*) rather than owned by
an ātman. The **store-consciousness (*ālaya-vijñāna*)** of the Yogācāra school is a close functional
cousin of *chitta* — a substratum carrying karmic "seeds" (*bīja*) that ripen as later experience —
i.e. **another theory of the subconscious**, but with *no* permanent self behind it. Holding Vedānta
and Abhidharma side by side frames the live disagreement: **is consciousness a constant ground
(turīya) or a process (stream)?** — the very question modern consciousness science still debates.

## 8. The consciousness mantras (the *mahāvākyas* and kin)

The "great sayings" assert the identity of awareness with the Absolute — the metaphysical capstone of
the whole psychology:

| Mantra | IAST | Meaning | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| प्रज्ञानं ब्रह्म | *prajñānaṃ brahma* | "**Consciousness is Brahman.**" | Aitareya Up. 3.1.3 (Ṛgveda) |
| अहं ब्रह्मास्मि | *ahaṃ brahmāsmi* | "**I am Brahman.**" | Bṛhadāraṇyaka Up. 1.4.10 (Yajurveda) |
| तत्त्वमसि | *tat tvam asi* | "**That thou art.**" | Chāndogya Up. 6.8.7 (Sāmaveda) |
| अयमात्मा ब्रह्म | *ayam ātmā brahma* | "**This Self is Brahman.**" | Māṇḍūkya Up. 2 (Atharvaveda) |
| सर्वं खल्विदं ब्रह्म | *sarvaṃ khalv idaṃ brahma* | "**All this is verily Brahman.**" | Chāndogya Up. 3.14.1 |
| नेति नेति | *neti neti* | "**Not this, not this**" (awareness is none of its objects) | Bṛhadāraṇyaka Up. 2.3.6 |

The thread: **what you fundamentally are is the witnessing consciousness (chit)**, not any faculty of
the inner instrument (manas/buddhi/ahaṃkāra/chitta) and not any state (waking/dream/sleep). The
faculties and states are *modifications known by* awareness; awareness itself is *turīya*.

## 9. The crosswalk — ancient functional model ↔ modern concepts

|---|---|---|
| **manas** | sensory coordination, intention/doubt, attention-gate | sensorimotor integration; attention; working buffer |
| **buddhi** | discrimination, decision, judgment | executive function; decision-making |
| **ahaṃkāra** | the "I-maker," appropriation | self-model; sense of agency/ownership |
| **chitta** | store of impressions; mind-field | long-term & implicit memory; the unconscious |

## 10. The honest bottom line

1. **Philosophy of mind:** the **Upaniṣads + Sāṃkhya + Yoga** give a genuinely deep, *structured*
   model — a four-fold cognitive architecture (*antaḥkaraṇa*), a clean split between **processing**
   (prakṛti/mind) and **awareness** (puruṣa/chit), a four-state theory of consciousness, and a real
   theory of the **subconscious** (chitta/saṃskāra/vāsanā).
2. **Dreams & sleep:** the **Māṇḍūkya** and **Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.3** texts model dreaming as endogenous
   world-simulation from memory and deep sleep as contentless non-dual awareness — sophisticated
   phenomenology.
3. **Brain anatomy:** **Āyurveda (Suśruta/Charaka)** supplies the real tissue, vital points, and
   surgery, and names the **brain (mastiṣka)** and **nervous tissue (majjā)** — but locates *mind/
   consciousness* often in the **heart**, and offers **no functional brain map.**
4. **Subtle body:** the **chakra/nāḍī** system is a **phenomenological** map of inner experience; its
   neuro-correlations are modern interpretation.
5. **Therefore:** the tradition's lasting gift is a **functional model of mind**, frequently
   surprising in its structure — while the detailed account of *how the brain physically works* is a
   product of **modern science**, not these texts.

## Appendix B — Sources & a note on transliteration

- **Upaniṣads:** Māṇḍūkya (+ Gauḍapāda's *Kārikā*), Kaṭha, Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya, Aitareya,
  **Praśna** (esp. 4, sleep/dream), Maitrī (standard editions; Olivelle's *Early Upaniṣads* and
  *Upaniṣads*, OUP, for English).
- **Vedic Saṃhitā:** Śukla Yajurveda, Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā 34 (Śiva-Saṅkalpa Sūkta).
- **Sāṃkhya:** *Sāṃkhya Kārikā* of Īśvarakṛṣṇa (with Gauḍapāda-bhāṣya).
- **Yoga:** *Pātañjala Yoga Sūtra* (with Vyāsa-bhāṣya).
- **Nyāya–Vaiśeṣika:** Gautama, *Nyāya Sūtra* (1.1.16 on manas); Kaṇāda, *Vaiśeṣika Sūtra*.
- **Advaita Vedānta:** Śaṅkara's *bhāṣyas*; *Vedānta-paribhāṣā* (Dharmarāja) on *antaḥkaraṇa-vṛtti*
  and *cidābhāsa* (reflection theory).
- **Buddhist:** *Abhidharma* (Vasubandhu's *Abhidharmakośa*); Yogācāra *ālaya-vijñāna* (store-
  consciousness) — included as the principled "no-Self / stream" contrast.
- **Āyurveda:** *Charaka Saṃhitā* (Sūtra/Śārīra-sthāna) and *Suśruta Saṃhitā* (Sūtra/Śārīra-sthāna).
- **Tantra/Haṭha:** *Ṣaṭ-cakra-nirūpaṇa* (Pūrṇānanda) — see Woodroffe, *The Serpent Power*;
  *Haṭha-yoga-pradīpikā*; *Gheraṇḍa-/Śiva-Saṃhitā*.
- **Bhagavad Gītā:** chs. 3, 6, 13, 15, 18.
